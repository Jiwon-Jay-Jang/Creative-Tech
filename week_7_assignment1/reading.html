1-1. We are now in transition from an object-oriented to a systems-oriented culture. Here change emanates, not from things, but from the way things are done.

I think this is a powerful sentence that captures the current trends in technology and design. The phrase 'the way things are done' is particularly impactful, as it highlights the role of IoT devices and a connected society in generating large amounts of data. Objects that only serve their own function no longer hold much significance; instead, dynamic interactions and experiences where objects and people, as well as objects and other objects, communicate and influence each other, have become essential.

2-1. Prototypes provide the means for examining design problems and evaluating solutions.

It was such an interesting sentence. I had never thought of prototypes as a way to examine design problems. I always thought problem identification was done through user-centered design methodologies, but now that I think about it, I've discovered many practical design issues while working with prototypes, and I still find new ones even now.And issues related to algorithms and usability are also design problems. I have mainly focused on social issues, but this statement has broadened my perspective on what constitutes a "design problem" and has challenged my preconceived notions.

2-2. Finally, prototypes are not self explanatory: looks can be deceiving. Clarifying what aspects of a prototype correspond to the eventual artifact—and what don’t—is a key part of successful prototyping. Prototypes themselves do not necessarily communicate their purpose; it is up to the designer to prepare an audience for viewing a prototype.

Yes, conveying the purpose and intent of a design through prototypes is crucial for designers. When ambition grows, it often leads to adding too many elements to the design, which can dilute its clarity. Prototyping should focus on clearly defining and visually expressing only the most essential aspects of the final product. I believe the key lies in the process of minimalization—removing unnecessary elements to enhance communication and ensure the design's core message is effectively delivered.

2-3. A prototype may explore questions or design options in one, two, or all three dimensions of the model: role, look and feel, and implementation.

Prototyping serves as a powerful tool that clearly conveys the purpose and intent of a design. It bridges the gaps between role, usability, aesthetics, and the actual implementation, facilitating effective communication with collaborators. By showcasing the essence of a design, prototyping becomes a compelling method to demonstrate its objectives and rationale.
